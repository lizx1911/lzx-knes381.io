The Impact of Force Versus Cadence on VO2 During a Step Incremental Test

Zhixuan Li.

Experimental display by others

Introduction

-Research Question: What is the effect of force versus cadence on VO2 during a step incremental test?
-In this experiment we manipulated the force and cadence which results in different VO2max at given power output
-Most accurate method to assess fitness is through measuring the VO2MAX
-Step incremental test is used to asses the VO2MAX

Introduction content

-Purpose of this study is to examine the difference in significance between force and cadence
-Controversies through studies in performance differences between high cadence versus high force
-Conflicting results from factors such as study designs, participant population, and methods used to assess VO2.

Rationale For the Study

-Practical Implications for Coaches and Athletes
-Optimal combination for Force and Cadence
-Guideline for enhancing performance and endurance capacities

Research Question and Hypothesis

-What is the effect of force versus cadence on VO2 during a step incremental test?
-Predicted that a high force steady cadence exercise would result in greater VO2.
-More force output leads to more activation and recruitment of muscle fibers which leads to greater energy demand and thus, greater oxygen demand (Millet et al., 2002)

Data Collection: Measures and Materials

-Bicycle ergometer (Ergoline GmbH; www.ergoline.com)
-Heart rate monitor (Polar Global; www.polar.com)
        -Heart rate
                -Shows stress on CV system
-Metabolic testing cart (Parvo Medics TrueOne 2400; www.parvo.com)
        -And all necessary accessories such as mask, hose, etc.
        -VO2
                -Metabolic demands of exercise
                -Muscle fibre types
-Modified RPE scale (Borg, 1970)
        -RPE (self-reported fatigue)
                -Central fatigue and pain

Data Collection: Procedures

-Manipulation tests
        -PO is same in each stage for both protocol A and B
        -Reached using cadence or load
-3 parts:
        -Baseline testing using maximal RAMP cycling test to determine maximum power output
        -Cadence-manipulation step-incremental cycling test
                -Load on the bike stays the same at every PO; cadence increases in three steps
        -Load-manipulation step-incremental cycling test
                -Cadence stays the same at every PO; load increases in three steps

Preliminary Results

-Higher VO2 in cadence manipulation - why?
        -Muscle fibre types for high-velocity tasks vs high-load (Plotkin et al., 2021)
                -Type IIx vs IIa
        -Rate of ventilation and rate of exercise task
-Intersection between cadence and load manipulation
        -“Sweet spot” for force-velocity relationship

Limitations

-This graph shows how there is a dissociation between VO2 during a RAMP test and constant load exercising, which is similar to the STEP test that we performed"
-At the same power output, a constant load exercise would require more VO2 compared to during a RAMP test, therefore our participant was seen to fatigue earlier, and unable to continue into stage 3 during our experiment"

Modifications

-Allocate more days for the experiment
-Recruit larger sample size
        -Population - untrained individuals aged 20-25
-Variables unchanged
        -Use of GET instead of PO max in RIT
-Control external variables to the best ability
        -Body weight/composition
        -Hydration status
        -Nutrition/Energy levels
-Protocol:
        -Determine exercise thresholds and domain
        -Prescribe exercise under the individual’s GET
